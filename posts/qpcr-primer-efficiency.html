Is your qPCR primer pair efficient?

The 2^-ΔΔCt method assumes your primers double the product every cycle. A standard curve tells you whether they actually do.

20 June 2026 · 1 min read

Relative quantification by the 2^-ΔΔCt method rests on one assumption: that the target and reference amplicons both roughly double every cycle, that is, that the primers are about 100% efficient. If they are not, fold-change estimates drift, and the error grows with the difference in Ct. A dilution-series standard curve is the standard way to check.

Run the standard curve

Prepare a series of template dilutions spanning several orders of magnitude, for example five tenfold steps, and run each in replicate. Plot the mean Ct against the base-ten logarithm of the template amount. The points should fall on a straight line.

Read the slope

Efficiency comes from the slope: a slope of −3.32 means the amount multiplies by ten every 3.32 cycles, which is exactly a doubling each cycle, or 100% efficiency. The general relationship is efficiency = 10^(−1/slope) − 1. A steeper slope means lower efficiency, a shallower slope means higher.

What counts as acceptable

Most laboratories accept a slope between −3.1 and −3.6, which is roughly 90 to 110% efficiency, together with an R² of at least 0.98 for a good linear fit. Outside that window, the assay needs attention.

If it fails

Low efficiency usually points to primer design or reaction conditions: check for secondary structure and primer-dimers, re-optimise the annealing temperature and primer concentration, and confirm the dilution series was made accurately, since a pipetting error in the standards shows up as a bad slope.
