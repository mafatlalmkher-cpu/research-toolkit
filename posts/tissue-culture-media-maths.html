Mixing a tissue-culture medium without arithmetic mistakes

Most media errors are not biology, they are arithmetic. Concentrated stocks and clear units remove the slips.

20 June 2026 · 2 min read

A plant tissue culture medium is mostly weighing and dissolving, and the step that goes wrong most often is the plant growth regulators. They are used at milligram-per-litre or micromolar concentrations, far too low to weigh out per batch, so they are added from concentrated stocks. Getting the stock and the addition right is simple arithmetic, but it is easy to slip.

Make stocks once, use them many times

Prepare each regulator as a concentrated stock, for example 1 mg per millilitre, and store it cold and dark. A 1 mg per millilitre stock is convenient because one millilitre gives 1 mg per litre in a one-litre batch. Weighing a few milligrams once, accurately, beats weighing fractions of a milligram for every medium.

Decide whether you are working in mg/L or µM

Recipes quote regulators in either mg per litre or micromolar. The two are not interchangeable without the molecular weight, because a milligram of one regulator is a different number of molecules than a milligram of another. When you compare doses across regulators, or reproduce a paper that uses molar units, convert with the molecular weight: µM = mg/L × 1000 ÷ MW.

Work out how much stock to add

The volume of stock to add is the amount you need divided by the stock strength: target concentration times batch volume, divided by the stock concentration. Keeping the units consistent is the whole game.

Dissolve and sterilise correctly

Most regulators do not dissolve in water alone. Auxins such as IAA, IBA, NAA, and 2,4-D go into a little 1 N NaOH or ethanol; cytokinins such as BA and kinetin go into dilute acid or base; thidiazuron needs DMSO. Some, including GA₃, zeatin, and abscisic acid, are heat-labile and must be filter-sterilised and added after autoclaving rather than autoclaved with the medium.
