Literature workflow

Free tools for your whole review

A literature review runs through about ten stages. This toolkit covers some of them; for the rest, free tools already do the job well. Each stage below gives a few options, a short comparison, and a clear default, so you can choose without guessing.

One rule throughout: a tool you already know beats a better one you would have to relearn in the middle of a project. Switch only to escape a paywall, to gain offline access you lack, or to stop losing data, and switch between projects, not during one. Where a stage says "stick with what you have," that is deliberate advice to save your time.

1. Register your protocol

Recording your plan before you start screening protects the review from later second-guessing and is expected by most journals.

ToolCostRunsBest for
PROSPEROFreeWebThe standard register for reviews with a health-related outcome. Approval in a few days.
OSF RegistriesFreeWebAny field, including plant science and ecology where PROSPERO does not fit. Immediate.
INPLASY~US$70WebSame-day registration with a DOI, only if you need speed and can pay the fee.

Default: PROSPERO if your review has a health outcome, otherwise OSF Registries (free, any topic). Register before formal screening; even a small review benefits.

2. Build your search

Turn your question into correct, reproducible Boolean queries for several databases.

ToolCostRunsBest for
QueryBeein this toolkitFreeWeb / offlineBuild correctly fielded queries for ten databases from concept blocks. Start here.
Polyglot Search TranslatorFreeWebTranslate one finished query into other database syntaxes, or check a translation.
litsearchrFreeR installTerm discovery and search building in R, if you are comfortable coding.

Default: QueryBee to build, Polyglot to double-check a translation. Stick with what you have if a librarian already gave you a validated string.

3. Manage your references

A reference manager stores records and PDFs, removes duplicates, and inserts citations as you write. It is the backbone the other stages feed into.

ToolCostRuns / offlineBest for
ZoteroFree, open sourceDesktop + web + phone, offlineThe default if you have no tool. Private, no lock-in, 300 MB free file storage.
Mendeley Reference ManagerFreeDesktop + web, syncThe current Mendeley. Fits people already in the Scopus and Elsevier world. 2 GB.
Mendeley DesktopFree, existing usersDesktop, offlineMaintenance only and no new downloads, but it keeps working. Keep it if you use it; move to Reference Manager in time.
EndNotePaid (~US$250; edu ~$205)Desktop, offlineLarge libraries and heavy citing. Worth it mainly when your institution provides it free.
RefWorksInstitution onlyWebUse only if your library already pays. Some are dropping it, so export your library now and then.

Default: Zotero if you are starting fresh. Already on Mendeley or EndNote and content: keep it, and do not switch mid-review.

4. Find the free full text

Most paywalled papers have a legal open-access copy somewhere. These tools surface it, which matters most when you have no journal subscriptions.

ToolCostRunsBest for
UnpaywallFreeBrowser add-onOne click to a legal open-access copy on any journal page. Install this first.
EndNote ClickFreeBrowser add-onFinds PDFs across PubMed, Web of Science, and 20,000 sites. Complements Unpaywall.
CORE and Europe PMCFreeWebSearch open repositories directly when an add-on finds nothing.
Library / interlibrary loanFree to youn/aFor anything still locked, ask your library. Interlibrary loan is free at most institutions.

Default: install Unpaywall and EndNote Click together; they cover different sources. This is the single biggest help if you lack subscriptions.

5. De-duplicate

Searching several databases collects the same paper many times. Remove duplicates before you read, not after.

ToolCostRunsBest for
De-duplicatorin this toolkitFreeWeb / offlineMerge RIS, BibTeX, and CSV and remove duplicates by DOI and fuzzy title. Start here.
SR-Accelerator DeduplicatorFreeWebA web alternative with a different matching method, useful for a second pass.
Zotero or MendeleyFreeDesktopBoth de-duplicate within a library, handy if your records already live there.

Default: the toolkit de-duplicator, or Zotero if your records are already in it. Always de-duplicate before screening.

6. Screen titles and abstracts

Decide which records to keep. Dedicated tools do this better than a single web page, so the toolkit recommends them.

ToolCostRunsBest for
CatchiiFree, no accountWeb / phone, offlineNo install. The closest fit for limited bandwidth. The default for most people.
ASReviewFree, open sourceLocal installMachine-learning prioritisation that saves time on large sets.
RayyanFree tier (account)WebTwo-reviewer blind screening with conflict resolution.

Default: Catchii. See the toolkit's screening guide for the full comparison and links.

7. Chase citations (snowballing)

Following the references and citations of the papers you already have catches studies your keyword search missed.

ToolCostRunsBest for
CitationchaserFreeWebBackward and forward citation chasing built for systematic reviews, exports to merge with your search. Start here.
Research RabbitFreeWebVisual "papers like this" discovery for exploring a field early on.
Connected PapersFree tier (5 graphs/mo)WebOne-off citation maps, fine within the monthly free limit.

Default: Citationchaser for a systematic review, because its output is transparent and merges with your records. Research Rabbit for early exploration.

8. Assess risk of bias

Judge how much to trust each included study, then turn the judgements into the figures journals expect.

ToolCostRunsBest for
RoB 2 / ROBINS-IFreeExcel / templatesCochrane's standard tools: RoB 2 for randomised trials, ROBINS-I for non-randomised studies.
robvisFreeWebTurns your assessments into the traffic-light and summary plots for the manuscript.

Default: the Cochrane tool that matches your study design, then robvis for the figure. No paid tool is needed here.

9. Meta-analysis and synthesis

Combine the numbers across studies. This is a statistics job; do it in a real stats tool, not a query builder.

ToolCostRunsBest for
jamoviFree, open sourceDesktop, offlinePoint-and-click meta-analysis (MAJOR module). The easiest if you do not code.
JASPFree, open sourceDesktop, offlineSimilar to jamovi, with classical and Bayesian meta-analysis.
Meta-EssentialsFreeExcelA set of workbooks if you would rather stay in a spreadsheet.
R (metafor)FreeR installThe most flexible option, for those comfortable writing code.
RevMan WebFree in Research4Life countries and for Cochrane reviews; else from ~£80/yrWebThe full Cochrane workflow. Free for many low-income-country researchers.

Default: jamovi or JASP for a clear interface and no coding. If you are in a Research4Life country, RevMan Web is free, so it is worth learning.

10. Report it (PRISMA)

Show that your review is complete and reproducible. The flow diagram and checklists are how reviewers check that.

ToolCostRunsBest for
PRISMA flow diagramin this toolkitFreeWeb / offlineBuild the PRISMA 2020 flow diagram from your counts and export it. Start here.
PRISMA 2020 + PRISMA-SFreeWeb / PDFThe item checklist and the search-reporting checklist; fill them alongside your manuscript.
EQUATOR NetworkFreeWebFinds the correct reporting guideline for any study type, not only reviews.

Default: the toolkit's PRISMA flow diagram plus the PRISMA 2020 checklist. Use EQUATOR to confirm you are following the right guideline.
