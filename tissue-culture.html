How much stock to add

Volume of stock = (target mg·L⁻¹ × medium L) ÷ stock mg·mL⁻¹. A 1 mg·mL⁻¹ stock is convenient: 1 mL gives 1 mg·L⁻¹ in 1 L of medium.

µM = mg·L⁻¹ × 1000 ÷ MW. mg·L⁻¹ = µM × MW ÷ 1000.

Prepare a stock solution

How much to weigh for a stock, and how to dissolve it.

–

Weigh the shown mass, dissolve in the minimum volume of the indicated solvent, then make up to the stock volume with water. Store cold and dark; heat-labile regulators should be filter-sterilised rather than autoclaved.

Tips that prevent a failed batch

Gellan needs divalent cations. In low-salt media (½MS, WPM, White) gellan may not set. Supply a little Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺, or blend with agar.
Acid and over-autoclaving weaken gels. Agar hydrolyses at low pH and with prolonged heat. Adjust pH to 5.6–5.8 before autoclaving and do not sterilise longer than the volume needs.
Soft gel and liquid contact cause hyperhydricity (glassy, water-soaked shoots). Firmer gel, ventilated closures, and lower cytokinin reduce it.

Activated charcoal and high sugar lower effective gel strength, so raise the gelling agent a little when you use them. Water quality matters: hard tap water adds uncontrolled cations. Use RO or distilled water where you can.

Acclimatisation (hardening off)

Plantlets grown in vitro have a thin cuticle, stomata that do not close properly, and little photosynthetic capacity, in a vessel near 100% humidity. Moving them out is where many protocols lose their plants, so treat it as a stage with its own data, not an afterthought.

A working sequence

  1. A day or two before transfer, loosen or open the closures and lower the light to begin adjustment.
  2. Lift the plantlets and wash all gelling agent from the roots in clean water; residue harbours microbes and rots roots.
  3. For fragile species, a brief dip in a broad-spectrum fungicide is optional.
  4. Plant in a sterile, low-nutrient, free-draining substrate (cocopeat:perlite 1:1, peat:vermiculite, or sand mixes). Avoid garden soil.
  5. Hold humidity high at first (90–95%) with a propagator lid, mist, or polytunnel, in bright shade rather than direct sun.
  6. Reduce humidity gradually over 2–4 weeks by venting a little more each day, so the cuticle and stomata become functional.
  7. Start a weak feed (¼-strength nutrients) after the first week or two, once new growth appears.
  8. Raise the light in steps; move to ambient conditions only after new, ex vitro leaves have hardened.

Tricks worth trying

  • Ex vitro rooting: root microcuttings directly in substrate with an auxin dip, hardening and rooting in one step.
  • Bottom heat at 22–25 °C speeds rooting and establishment.
  • Antitranspirant sprays cut early water loss for difficult species.
  • Stagger the venting: a little more air each day beats one large drop in humidity.

What to avoid

  • Gel left on roots, which rots them and seeds contamination.
  • Sudden low humidity or direct sun, which desiccates in vitro leaves.
  • Full-strength fertiliser early, which burns weak roots.
  • Waterlogged, reused, or non-sterile substrate, which invites damping-off.
Report it so it counts. Give the substrate, the humidity regime and how it was reduced, light, temperature, duration, and survival percentage with the number of plants at a stated time point. A multiplication protocol without ex vitro survival data is incomplete.

How to start, what to avoid, what to target

Plant Cell, Tissue and Organ Culture (PCTOC) and its sister journals reward studies that resolve a real bottleneck and report enough to be repeated. The choices below decide both the biology and whether the work is publishable.

How to start

  • Fix the objective first. Rapid multiplication, de novo regeneration, virus elimination, somatic embryogenesis, conservation, or transformation. The aim sets the explant, the medium, and the data a reader expects.
  • Match the explant to the aim. Nodal segments and shoot tips for clonal multiplication and fidelity; meristem tips for virus elimination; leaf, internode, or immature tissue for callus and regeneration; anthers or microspores for haploids.
  • Build a sterilisation protocol and report its numbers, the contamination and survival rates, not only the surviving clean cultures.
  • Pick a basal medium from the comparison tab, then screen growth regulators across a justified dose range with a regulator-free control.
  • Design replication in from the start: the independent explant as the unit, repeated in time, with enough replicates for the test you plan to run.

What to avoid

  • Pseudoreplication: treating shoots from one explant, or jars from one vessel batch, as independent replicates.
  • One genotype, general claim: a result from a single cultivar written as if it held for the species.
  • Silent hyperhydricity and somaclonal variation: not checking for them, or not reporting them.
  • Missing acclimatisation: stopping at in vitro rooting with no ex vitro survival.
  • An incompletely specified medium: regulators in mg·L⁻¹ only or µM only, no gelling agent, no pH, no sterilisation detail.
  • No fidelity check behind a clonal-propagation claim.

What to target

  • Go past "another species on MS + BA." Add a mechanism, a comparison that settles a real difficulty, or a method others can transfer.
  • Include genetic fidelity (SSR, ISSR, SCoT, or flow cytometry) for micropropagation, and acclimatisation with survival data.
  • Report design and statistics in full so the result can be reproduced.
  • State the advance in the first paragraph: the problem, the gap, and what this study adds.
Before you submit, run the work through the forensic manuscript checklist, the same checks an editor and reviewer will apply.
